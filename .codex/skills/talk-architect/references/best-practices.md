# Recent Academic Talk Best Practices

This file distills recent guidance relevant to academic talks. Use it to justify planning decisions and to keep the skill aligned with current practice.

## What audiences usually prefer

Across recent university and conference guidance, audiences prefer:
- a clear thesis early
- a strong answer to "why should I care?"
- one main point per slide
- slide titles that state the point, not just the topic
- visuals that match the current spoken focus
- only the strongest evidence in the main flow
- notes and delivery carrying detail instead of overloaded slides
- intentional backup slides for Q&A and extra detail

Audiences tend to disengage when:
- the speaker follows paper section order without a live narrative
- the contribution appears too late
- one slide contains multiple competing claims or visual focal points
- figures are copied from papers without pruning for oral delivery
- text is too small, dense, or hard to decode in real time

## Operational Rules To Apply

### 1. Design the flow before the slides
A recent George Washington University guide recommends mapping the flow first, then checking whether the slide order itself tells the right story.

Apply this as:
- lock the story arc before slide drafting
- use slide order to reflect logic, not manuscript structure

Source:
- George Washington University, "Tips for Creating and Delivering Effective Slide Presentations," February 2025
  https://cfe.smhs.gwu.edu/sites/g/files/zaskib506/files/2025-02/tips_for_creating_and_delivering_effective_slide_presentations.pdf

### 2. One main message per slide
Recent university guidance consistently recommends one idea or one major point per slide because audiences process talks in real time, not at reading speed.

Apply this as:
- each slide must support a single claim
- split crowded slides even when the paper used one figure panel

Sources:
- George Washington University, February 2025
- Penn State Hancock Lab, "Presentation Basics," March 2024
  https://www.hancocklab.org/poster-and-presentation-tips

### 3. Use active, conclusion-driven titles
Recent conference and university guidance favors titles that state the slide's takeaway instead of labels like `Method`, `Results`, or `Ablation`.

Apply this as:
- turn section labels into claims
- write titles that a listener could repeat as the slide's takeaway

Sources:
- University of Wisconsin-Madison, "Presentation Best Practices," updated August 2025
  https://brand.wisc.edu/content/uploads/2025/08/presentation-best-practices.pdf
- WSC 2025 speaker guidance emphasizing active slide titles
  https://www.informs.org/Recognizing-Excellence/Community-Prizes-and-Awards/Doing-Good-with-Good-OR-Prize/Past-Recipients

### 4. Slides are visual aids, not scripts
Recent talk guides repeatedly stress that spoken detail belongs in delivery or notes, not on-screen prose.

Apply this as:
- keep notes richer than slides
- avoid projecting manuscript-style paragraphs
- use slides to focus attention, not to store the full wording

Sources:
- University of Rochester, "Mastering Scientific Presentations," March 3, 2025
  https://www.urmc.rochester.edu/education/graduate/myhub-blog/march-03-2025/mastering-scientific-presentations-powerpoint-best-practices-for-graduate-trainees
- University of Wisconsin-Madison, August 2025

### 5. Time should constrain slide count
Recent guidance commonly suggests roughly one slide per minute, with denser expert talks sometimes running slightly faster and broader talks slightly slower.

Apply this as:
- conference default around 0.8-1.2 slides per minute
- seminar and broad-audience talks can run slower
- reserve backup slides instead of overfilling the main deck

Sources:
- University of Rochester, March 2025
- Penn State Hancock Lab, March 2024
- Visalia 2025 presenter guidance
  https://visalia2025.tws-west.org/guidelines-for-posters-and-presentations/

### 6. Reduce chart detail to the current point
Recent speaker guidance recommends cutting chart elements that are not needed for the point being discussed.

Apply this as:
- show only the evidence needed for the current claim
- move dense tables and secondary ablations to backup
- annotate or redraw figures for oral clarity rather than pasting paper figures raw

Source:
- George Washington University, February 2025

### 7. Accessibility is part of quality
Recent conference accessibility rules reinforce that good talks need readable text, strong contrast, and non-color-only encoding.

Apply this as:
- keep text large enough for the back of the room
- ensure takeaways are stated in words, not only in visuals
- avoid color-only distinctions in figures and diagrams

Source:
- UbiComp/ISWC 2025 accessibility guidance
  https://ubicomp.hosting.acm.org/ubicompiswc2025_wp/authors/accessibility-guidelines/

### 8. Wider audiences need more framing
Broader talks, especially candidate-style talks, must serve experts and non-experts at once.

Apply this as:
- define audience distance explicitly
- increase stakes, framing, and agenda-level synthesis for job-talk or broad seminar contexts

Source:
- University of Washington faculty search handbook
  https://education.uw.edu/sites/default/files/mycoe/facstaff/faculty/faculty-search-handbook.pdf

## Practical Planning Heuristics

Use these by default unless the user asks otherwise:
- get to the problem and contribution early
- prefer claim -> evidence -> interpretation within a slide
- keep one visual focus per reveal
- put likely Q&A material into backup on purpose
- if a slide cannot be summarized as one sentence, it probably contains more than one idea
